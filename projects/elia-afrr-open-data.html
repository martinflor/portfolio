Context

This page is a first pass at exploring automatic Frequency Restoration Reserve (aFRR) using ELIA Open Data. The goal is to move from “looking at prices” to extracting bidding-relevant signals from historical behavior: volume-weighted prices, acceptance probabilities, and a simple bid recommendation approach.

Data sources

aFRR energy prices

ELIA Open Data provides energy activation prices through two datasets:

  • ods064 — until 2024-05-21
  • ods166 — from 2024-05-22 onward

aFRR capacity prices & volumes

Capacity-related results (prices + awarded volumes) come from:

  • ods125 — awarded capacity prices & awarded volumes
Market rule that matters

aFRR capacity prices are pay-as-bid (not pay-as-cleared). That means each accepted bid is paid at its own bid price, which makes acceptance probability and bid selection central to strategy design.

Volume-weighted capacity prices

Capacity prices can be noisy if volumes fluctuate. A common fix is to compute volume-weighted prices:

\[ P_{\text{vw}} = \frac{\sum_i P_i \cdot V_i}{\sum_i V_i} \]

where \(P_i\) is the awarded capacity price and \(V_i\) the awarded volume (for a given time bucket and direction). This provides a more representative view of “what the system actually paid” when volumes vary.

What I look for in this chart

  • Skew & spikes: heavy-tailed behavior is typical — beware averages.
  • UP vs DOWN asymmetry: different scarcity dynamics can show up clearly.
  • Regime changes: clusters of spikes can indicate persistent tightness.

Probability of being accepted vs bid price

With pay-as-bid, the key question becomes: “If I bid at price \(p\), what is my probability of acceptance?”

We estimate:

\[ \mathbb{P}(\text{accept} \mid p) \]

and segment it by time-of-day blocks (e.g., 00–04, 04–08, …). In most datasets, the curve typically decays non-linearly: higher bid prices reduce acceptance probability sharply beyond a certain range.

Interpretation

  • Time-of-day matters: scarcity and competition differ by block.
  • Non-linear effect: a small price increase can disproportionately reduce acceptance.
  • Strategic takeaway: the same bid can be “good” in one block and “too high” in another.

Recommended bid price using historical expected value

Acceptance probability alone isn’t enough: a very low bid might be accepted almost always, but yields little revenue. A simple historical decision rule is to maximize a proxy for expected value:

\[ \text{EV}(p) = p \cdot \mathbb{P}(\text{accept} \mid p) \]

The best historical bid (per block, direction) is the \(p\) that maximizes \(\text{EV}(p)\), optionally adjusted for risk preferences (e.g., prefer higher acceptance vs higher unit price).

A practical recommendation workflow

1) Build \(\mathbb{P}(\text{accept} \mid p)\) per block

From historical awarded bids, compute acceptance frequency in price bins (or a smoothed curve).

2) Compute \(\text{EV}(p)\) and locate the maximum

Use \(\text{EV}(p)=p \cdot \mathbb{P}(\text{accept} \mid p)\) to find a baseline bid \(p^\*\).

3) Apply a risk adjustment

If you want higher acceptance, bid slightly below \(p^\*\). If you want higher unit value and can accept misses, bid slightly above \(p^\*\).

Important note

This is not a forecast. It’s a historical decision support baseline. Market conditions change — you should update curves regularly and add context signals if possible.

Limitations & next steps

Limitations

  • Assumes historical stationarity.
  • Doesn’t model structural changes explicitly.
  • Doesn’t separate participant-level strategies.

Next steps

  • Repeat for DOWN products.
  • Add conditioning on tightness / system indicators.
  • Link energy price regimes to capacity acceptance.
  • Turn “baseline” into a live-updating dashboard.
